Preface
This article explores eBay's innovative use of the Mercury platform to enhance personalized recommendations through Large Language Models (LLMs). Mercury, an agentic framework, enables scalable LLM-powered experiences by integrating Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) and a Listing Matching Engine, aligning LLM outputs with eBay's dynamic inventory. By focusing on reusability, scalability, and AI safety, Mercury allows eBay to deliver timely and relevant product recommendations, setting a new standard for AI-driven large scale e-commerce solutions.
Introduction
eBay, a global e-commerce platform with over two billion active listings, enables users to sell and buy virtually anything. This vast and diverse ecosystem generates an immense amount of unstructured data, from listings to user interactions. Making sense of this data to provide personalized recommendations is a significant challenge. Here is some of the team’s previous work on eBay recommendations systems: RecSys 2016 paper, RecSys 2021 paper, eBay Tech Blog 2022 post, eBay Tech Blog Jan 2023 post, eBay Tech Blog Oct 2023 post, Google Cloud Blog 2024, RecSys 2024 Workshop paper. The emergence of Large Language Models (LLMs) provides a transformative approach to addressing these challenges. LLMs have a broad range of applications and use cases at eBay, particularly in enhancing recommendations. Yet scaling these applications to serve hundreds of millions of customers across billions of item listings across numerous experiences is both challenging and costly.
To address these limitations of LLMs, we developed Mercury, an internal agentic framework and execution platform designed to facilitate the creation of LLM-powered experiences at scale. In the Recommendations org, our job is to generate relevant personalized item recommendations to our users. We will describe how we utilize Mercury, LLMs, RAG, and text-to-listing retrieval to enhance our recommender system. This article does make an assumption that you are somewhat familiar with Machine Learning, LLMs, and Recommendation Systems.
Background
Mercury started development concurrently with the rise of LLMs to meet eBay’s unique industrial-level scalability needs. It is an agentic framework that allows you to build LLM/GenAI-powered systems with some level of autonomy, adaptability, and goal-oriented behavior. This enables them to make somewhat independent decisions and interact dynamically with their environments to react to different situations. In Mercury, a unit of LLM work is structured as an "Agent," which allows us to build AI-powered capabilities with various levels of complexity and autonomy. An "Agent," in its simplest form, encapsulates an instruction (prompt) to a Generative Model (e.g., an LLM) along with transformation layers to augment the input data to the expected format and translate the model's output to an expected structure. The abstraction of the input and output data formats away from the internal implementation allows us to have multiple Agents that honor the same contract but differ in complexity, models, implementation, or optimization of quality vs. latency. This abstraction also allows the components to be plug-and-play, maintaining strong inter-agent compatibility and reusability. This enables customer teams to build complex systems by combining simple, well-defined parts of inputs and outputs, as you would with any other large-scale applications. Our approach not only speeds up development but also enhances the robustness of the solutions by following long-standing OOP engineering standards to build complex and scalable solutions through a series of well-defined components that can be individually upgraded or swapped out.
The OOP concepts are also applied within “Agents” to accommodate more intricate behaviors by linking together a sequence of steps or processes commonly referred to as “Chains.” Each of these sequences is also presented as an agent, enabling the creation of increasingly complex components while maintaining a consistent interface. This design ensures that engineers and applied researchers need only focus on inputs and outputs, without concerning themselves with the underlying workings. Similarly, this approach allows for the simplified development/orchestration of sophisticated and complex decision-making autonomous agents, as multiple agents can also be configured as trees or graphs to support more autonomous operations, as seen in frameworks like LangGraph. Furthermore, this lays the foundation for a very interesting future where all the Agents and capabilities we are building can self-assemble as a very large network of Agents using self-discovery and the existing defined Agent contracts. This will allow a more capable AI/LLM (than available now) that can truly be “autonomous” and plug into this network to use the various lesser Agents as tools to perform specialized tasks.

Mercury’s NRT execution platform is where things get interesting due to cost and latency requirements at eBay (as little as hundreds of ms). Most products and solutions built rely on some caching based solution and preprocessing. Our distributed queue-based message passing system alleviates the need to be tied to users activity pattern at eBay, which typically consists of peaks and valleys throughout the day. These peaks and valleys result in poor utilization of GPUs and allocated reserved capacity from external models. Our NRT execution platform smooths out this curve to create consistent and controllable throughput in aggregate across all use cases or per use case through an on-the-fly configuration mechanism for adjustment of priority. As typical with distributed queue based systems, it’s resilient, self healing, and has low maintenance needs as failed tasks are simply retried via our automated retry mechanism at some later time. Throughput aperture of parallel processing can also be controlled easily on-the-fly and any major service disruption issues mean that the processing will auto resume when the appropriate failures are recovered.
RAG In Mercury
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) is a well supported paradigm in Mercury, allowing us to enhance the capabilities of language models by integrating domain-specific information. This approach leverages the advanced in-context learning abilities of language models to comprehend and utilize the provided information effectively. As a result, RAG helps to generate accurate and contextually appropriate responses that the LLMs would otherwise be unable to produce independently, no matter how good the LLM is, because every LLM contains information only up to the training data date. For example, the original ChatGPT is only aware of world information up to September 2021. This hybrid approach allows eBay to deliver highly accurate, recent, and contextually relevant recommendations.
Since RAG is essentially an open book test for the LLM, access to vast relevant and current information that is structured and unstructured is the ultimate key and challenge. In Mercury we have gone to great lengths to integrate as many relevant sources as we can,such as user and listing information as well as vast amounts of scraped publicly available content from the internet that is updated periodically via Common Crawl. Moreover, Mercury has the ability to perform Google searches to incorporate real time data from the web to react and produce relevant recommendations for things such as trending topics and recent or upcoming releases along with access to external partner data for additional context and signals for trends outside of eBay. Each data source has its pros and cons in terms of latency, relevancy, and costs. Each use case and application built with Mercury is able to make the appropriate tradeoffs for its needs.

Mercury’s Listing Matching Engine
While the goal of our recommender system is to generate item recommendations, the LLM produces output text, so we need a way to map text to items. This is where Mercury’s Listing Matching Engine comes into play. Our Listing Matching Engine is built specifically to work with LLMs, integrating seamlessly into eBay’s ecosystem. Since LLMs don’t have access to specific current insights into eBay’s dynamic inventory of over two billion live items, we developed this engine to bridge the gap between LLM outputs and our dynamic marketplace to support low latency retrieval needs. Whether a user is being recommended products from the latest personalized trends or receives recommendations based on their current or previous shopping missions, the Listing Matching Engine connects LLM generated product ideas to live eBay listings, delivering a highly relevant shopping experience.
Text To Listing Retrieval
The Listing Matching Engine’s Text-to-Listing Retrieval phase dynamically matches each LLM-generated product name or search query with eBay’s current listings, combining several retrieval approaches to ensure highly relevant results. A pattern we developed uses a query expansion mechanism that can turn any topic into an expanded set of queries and products recommended by the LLM via its learned knowledge or its understanding of a topic via a RAG based approach. After filtering, the remaining listings are ranked according to a personalized model that tailors results to each user. The ranking process considers the user’s previous interactions, shopping behaviors, and preferences, ensuring that listings are directly aligned with their interests. The engine uses round-robin presentation to ensure fair visibility for each query, delivering a diverse mix of products tailored to user activity. Depending on the use case and deciding between quality vs latency requirements, we also have components to optimize the quality of retrieved items and can run for N steps or until some scoring threshold has been achieved. The optimized queries can be saved in our cache for future needs.
AI Safety And Security
At eBay, ensuring the responsible and safe use of AI is a top priority. To support this, Mercury offers access to a range of content moderation models and provides options to integrate LLM safety mechanisms wherever possible. Additionally, it includes internal models to detect and prevent prompt injection by malicious actors. This suite of tools empowers different use cases to select the most suitable solutions based on their specific needs, scale, and standards established by our Responsible AI, Content, and Legal teams.
Conclusion
In conclusion, eBay's integration of Large Language Models (LLMs) through the Mercury platform represents a significant advancement in enhancing personalized recommendation experiences on the eBay marketplace. By leveraging the capabilities of Mercury, eBay is able to add world knowledge encapsulated in the LLM and RAG systems to digest the vast unstructured data in seller listings, thereby delivering highly relevant and contextually accurate product recommendations to its users. Mercury's agentic framework, with its plug-and-play environment and intercompatible components, allows for rapid development and scalability, ensuring that these sophisticated solutions can meet the demands of eBay's global user base.The architecture's focus on reusability, ease of productionization, and built-in observability ensures that the solutions are robust, reliable, and maintainable. Moreover, the Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) paradigm and the Listing Matching Engine allow for a seamless integration of LLM outputs with eBay's dynamic marketplace, ensuring that users receive recommendations that are both timely and aligned with their interests.
Through these innovations, eBay is not only enhancing the shopping experience for its users but also setting a new standard for how e-commerce platforms can utilize advanced AI technologies to drive engagement and satisfaction at industrial scale. As eBay continues to refine and expand its use of LLMs, the potential for even more personalized and intuitive shopping experiences is immense, promising a future where technology and commerce are more closely aligned than ever before.
